1Prologue — Why Digital Filters?

A digital filter is a linear, time-invariant map from an input sequence $x[n]$ to an output sequence $y[n]$ — implemented as a recursion of multiplies and adds. Nothing more exotic than that. And yet, dressed in the z-transform and plotted on the unit circle, this family of maps covers every equaliser in every DAW, every anti-aliasing filter in every ADC, every reverb, every synth, every modem, every hearing aid.

Julius O. Smith III's Introduction to Digital Filters with Audio Applications — the W3K Publishing volume freely hosted at ccrma.stanford.edu/~jos/filters/ — is the textbook several generations of Stanford CCRMA students read before touching a sound. It privileges two ideas above all others: the transfer function $H(z)$, and the geometry of its poles and zeros on the complex plane. This companion is a browser-native set of experiments around exactly those two ideas.

$$y[n] \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{M} b_i\, x[n-i] \;-\; \sum_{j=1}^{N} a_j\, y[n-j]$$

Every filter in this guide is some instance of that difference equation. The entire intellectual apparatus — z-transforms, frequency response, stability, minimum phase, design by windowing or by bilinear transform — exists to help you pick the $\{a_j, b_i\}$.

2A Sketch of the History

Digital filtering is one of the rare engineering subjects whose central concepts were mostly in place before the computers that would execute them. By the time Cooley and Tukey's FFT arrived in 1965, the z-transform, the sampling theorem, and feedback-system theory were all twenty years old. The history of digital filters is therefore a history of porting continuous-domain instincts onto discrete time, and learning which ones survive.

1927
Harold S. Black (Bell Labs) invents the negative-feedback amplifier on a Lackawanna Ferry in August. Feedback trades gain for linearity and bandwidth and introduces the idea that a system is best analysed by a transfer function with poles and zeros.
1928 & 1933
Harry Nyquist and Vladimir Kotelnikov establish the sampling theorem — a bandlimited signal is fully determined by samples at twice its highest frequency. Claude Shannon formalises it in 1948. Discrete-time filtering becomes a well-posed question.
1945
Hendrik Bode's Network Analysis and Feedback Amplifier Design crystallises the log-magnitude / log-frequency plot and the minimum- phase / allpass decomposition — tools the digital world inherits whole.
1947-1952
Lotfi Zadeh and John Ragazzini at Columbia develop the z-transform for sampled-data control systems. Their 1952 paper "The analysis of sampled-data systems" fixes the notation still used today. The z-transform does for discrete LTI systems what Laplace does for continuous ones.
1965
Cooley & Tukey publish the FFT. The economics of frequency-domain analysis collapse from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N\log N)$, making overlap-save convolution and FFT-based filter design newly practical.
1966
James Kaiser publishes his eponymous window and the $(\beta, L)$ design formulas. Windowed-FIR design is now systematic: pick a stopband attenuation, compute $\beta$, compute $L$, truncate the ideal impulse response.
1968
Charles Rader describes the parametric biquad and digital resonator forms that every synthesiser and EQ plugin still uses. At Lincoln Laboratory, DSP is moving from batch processing to real time.
1969
Bernard Gold & Charles Rader publish Digital Processing of Signals — the first DSP textbook. It collects the z-transform, FIR/IIR design, quantisation analysis, and the first signal-flow graph treatments of DF-I and DF-II structures.
1972
Thomas Parks & James McClellan publish the Chebyshev-optimal (equiripple) FIR algorithm — the Parks-McClellan / Remez exchange method. For a given length and passband/stopband, no FIR filter has smaller peak error. Still the industry standard.
1975
Rabiner & Gold's Theory and Application of Digital Signal Processing and Oppenheim & Schafer's Digital Signal Processing both appear. The canon is set. A generation of engineers learn the field from these two volumes.
1985
Robert Bristow-Johnson's Audio EQ Cookbook (later published as a web document) spells out closed-form biquad coefficients for every audio EQ shape. Every DAW plugin ships a copy.
1988
Proakis & Manolakis's Digital Signal Processing: Principles, Algorithms, and Applications becomes the standard undergraduate text for the next two decades. Julius Smith's CCRMA notes evolve in parallel into the book this guide companions.
2000s-today
The plugin era. Every DAW ships biquads, linear-phase FIR EQ, Schroeder/Moorer reverbs, Butterworth / Chebyshev / elliptic anti-alias filters, oversampled nonlinearities, and learned filters. JOS's four online books (DFT, Digital Filters, Spectral, Physical) become the reference.
"The theory of digital filters is the theory of the z-transform, made concrete by the geometry of poles and zeros." — J. O. Smith III, Introduction to Digital Filters, paraphrased

3Signals, Systems, Linear Time-Invariance

A discrete-time signal is a function $x:\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{R}$. A discrete-time system maps one signal to another: $y = T\{x\}$. A system is linear if superposition holds; it is time-invariant if delaying the input by $k$ samples delays the output by $k$ samples. When both hold — LTI — the system is completely characterised by its impulse response $h[n] = T\{\delta[n]\}$.

$$y[n] \;=\; (h * x)[n] \;=\; \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} h[k]\,x[n-k]$$

That sum is convolution. It is the single most important operation in digital signal processing: every FIR filter is a convolution, and every IIR filter has a convolution representation in terms of its (infinite) impulse response. The rest of JOS's book is largely a study of clever parameterisations for $h[n]$.

Properties that will matter later

  • Causality. $h[n]=0$ for $n<0$. Required for real-time implementation.
  • Stability. $\sum_n |h[n]| < \infty$ (BIBO). Every bounded input gives a bounded output.
  • FIR. $h[n]$ has finite support. Always stable. Convolution is a direct sum.
  • IIR. $h[n]$ has infinite support. Implementable via a difference equation with feedback. Must be checked for stability.

A general LTI system whose impulse response satisfies a linear constant-coefficient difference equation is precisely the class every "filter" belongs to. The z-transform turns that class into an algebraic object you can design with.

4The z-Transform

The z-transform is the discrete-time analogue of the Laplace transform. It turns difference equations into algebraic equations, convolution into multiplication, and the question "what does this filter do?" into "where are its poles and zeros?"

$$X(z) \;=\; \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n]\, z^{-n}, \qquad z \in \mathbb{C}$$

The region of convergence (ROC) is the annulus of $|z|$ for which the sum converges. For a causal, stable system the ROC includes $|z|=1$ and extends outward — which is the same as saying all poles lie strictly inside the unit circle.

Difference equation ↔ rational transfer function

Take the z-transform of the difference equation $\sum_j a_j y[n-j] = \sum_i b_i x[n-i]$ with $a_0=1$. Using the shift property $x[n-k] \leftrightarrow z^{-k}X(z)$:

$$H(z) \;=\; \frac{Y(z)}{X(z)} \;=\; \frac{\sum_{i=0}^{M} b_i z^{-i}}{1 + \sum_{j=1}^{N} a_j z^{-j}} \;=\; \frac{b_0 \prod_i (1 - q_i z^{-1})}{\prod_j (1 - p_j z^{-1})}$$

The roots $q_i$ of the numerator are the zeros — frequencies the filter annihilates. The roots $p_j$ of the denominator are the poles — resonances. That is the whole geometric picture of the unit disk.

5Frequency Response, Phase, Group Delay

The unit circle is where digital filters earn their keep. Evaluating $H(z)$ at $z=e^{j\omega}$ gives the frequency response — a complex-valued function of normalised frequency. Its magnitude is what you hear as loudness-vs-pitch; its phase tells you how each frequency is delayed.

$$H(e^{j\omega}) \;=\; \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} h[n]\, e^{-j\omega n}$$

This is the DTFT of the impulse response. The magnitude response $|H(e^{j\omega})|$ and the phase response $\phi(\omega) = \angle H(e^{j\omega})$ are both real functions of $\omega \in [-\pi, \pi]$. Real-coefficient filters have conjugate-symmetric frequency response, so we only need to plot $\omega \in [0, \pi]$.

Group delay

The group delay is the negative derivative of phase with respect to frequency:

$$\tau_g(\omega) \;=\; -\,\frac{d\phi(\omega)}{d\omega}$$

If a narrowband signal centred at $\omega_0$ passes through the filter, its envelope is delayed by $\tau_g(\omega_0)$ samples. A linear-phase filter has constant group delay — every frequency is delayed equally, so the waveform shape of a transient is preserved. Every symmetric FIR filter is linear-phase. No causal IIR filter is, in general, linear-phase.

Triple panel: magnitude / phase / group delay

Aside: Bode and the birth of log-log plotting

Hendrik Bode's Network Analysis and Feedback Amplifier Design (1945) is the reason we plot $20\log_{10}|H|$ versus $\log\omega$. On that grid, rational transfer functions become piecewise-linear asymptotes, and the phase plot is a short table of lookup curves. Bode's minimum-phase theorem — for a minimum-phase system, magnitude and phase are related by a Hilbert transform — survives verbatim into the digital world and motivates Chapter 7's minimum-phase / allpass decomposition.

6Canonical Elementary Filters

Most useful filters are compositions of a handful of second-order (biquad) sections. JOS's book spends several chapters building intuition for these elementary shapes, because once you recognise them on the z-plane you can read any datasheet at a glance.

One-pole lowpass

$$H(z) \;=\; \frac{1-p}{1-p\,z^{-1}}, \qquad p \in (0,1)$$

One real pole at $z=p$. Gain is normalised so $H(1)=1$ (DC gain = 1). The -3 dB frequency is $\omega_c \approx 1-p$ for $p \to 1$.

One-zero lowpass (the moving average of 2)

$$H(z) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}(1 + z^{-1})$$

A single zero at $z=-1$ (Nyquist). Linear-phase, group delay = 1/2 sample.

Two-pole resonator

$$H(z) \;=\; \frac{1}{1 - 2r\cos(\omega_0)\,z^{-1} + r^2\,z^{-2}}$$

Conjugate pole pair at $r e^{\pm j\omega_0}$. Resonant frequency $\omega_0$; $Q$ increases as $r\to 1$. Impulse response is a decaying sinusoid $r^n \sin(\omega_0 n + \phi)$. This is the core of every digital synth resonator and every ringing physical model.

Second-order notch

$$H(z) \;=\; \frac{1 - 2\cos(\omega_0)\,z^{-1} + z^{-2}}{1 - 2r\cos(\omega_0)\,z^{-1} + r^2\,z^{-2}}$$

Zeros on the unit circle at $e^{\pm j\omega_0}$ kill frequency $\omega_0$ exactly. Poles just inside the circle at the same angle, $r<1$, control the notch width. As $r\to 1$ the notch narrows; as $r\to 0$ it broadens into a general band-stop.

Shelving and peaking (Bristow-Johnson biquad)

The RBJ Audio EQ Cookbook closed-forms compute $b_0, b_1, b_2, a_1, a_2$ given corner frequency $\omega_0 = 2\pi f_0/f_s$, quality $Q$, and gain in dB:

alpha = sin(w0) / (2 Q) A = 10^(dBgain/40) // for peaking and shelving // Peaking EQ: b0 = 1 + alpha * A b1 = -2 cos(w0) b2 = 1 - alpha * A a0 = 1 + alpha / A a1 = -2 cos(w0) a2 = 1 - alpha / A

7Poles, Zeros, Stability, Minimum Phase

Geometry on the unit disk encodes almost everything practical about a digital filter: stability is a pole-location question; minimum phase is a zero-location question; linear phase is a symmetry question on $h[n]$.

Stability

A causal LTI system is BIBO-stable iff every pole lies strictly inside the unit circle: $|p_j| < 1$ for all $j$. A pole on the circle ($|p|=1$) gives a marginally stable system; a pole outside ($|p|>1$) blows up. In the flagship demo in Chapter 4, drag any pole out past the circle to see the UNSTABLE indicator trigger and the impulse response diverge.

Minimum phase and allpass decomposition

A stable, causal filter is minimum phase if all its zeros also lie inside the unit circle. Minimum-phase filters have the smallest possible group delay among all causal filters with the same magnitude response, and they are invertible by a stable, causal filter (the inverse has poles where $H$ has zeros, all inside the disk).

Every rational $H(z)$ factorises uniquely as

$$H(z) \;=\; H_{\text{min}}(z)\, H_{\text{ap}}(z)$$

where $H_{\text{ap}}$ is an allpass filter — $|H_{\text{ap}}(e^{j\omega})|=1$ for all $\omega$. The allpass carries all the "extra" phase, the minimum-phase factor carries the magnitude. This is the discrete-time analogue of Bode's theorem. See also the companion MinimumMaximumPhaseFilters guide for a dedicated exploration.

Interactive: reflect a zero across the unit circle

If a zero at $q$ is reflected to its reciprocal conjugate $\bar{q}^{-1} = 1/\bar q$, the magnitude response is unchanged, but the phase response changes — exactly by the phase of an allpass. Drag the zero between $|q|<1$ and $|q|>1$ and watch: same magnitude, different phase.

FIR filter H(z) = (1 - q z^-1)(1 - q* z^-1). Left: z-plane. Right top: |H| — invariant under reflection. Right bottom: angle H — different for minimum vs maximum phase.
In audio, minimum-phase EQs (most analogue modelling plugins) have lower latency and preserve transient alignment; linear-phase EQs (symmetric FIR) have exact phase-invariance but introduce pre-ringing audible on drums. Allpass filters are the glue that lets you trade one for the other.

8Implementation Structures

Given a rational $H(z)$ there are many signal-flow graphs that compute the same difference equation. They differ in number of delays, arithmetic precision needed, sensitivity to coefficient quantisation, and parallelism on a real processor.

Direct Form I (DF-I)

Compute the feed-forward sum first, then the feedback. Uses $M+N$ delays. Safe numerically because there is no internal state that can overflow independently.

$$w[n] \;=\; \sum_i b_i x[n-i], \qquad y[n] \;=\; w[n] - \sum_j a_j y[n-j]$$

Direct Form II (DF-II)

Swap the order — first apply the all-pole part to produce an intermediate $v[n]$, then apply the all-zero part to $v[n]$. Needs only $\max(M,N)$ delays. Can overflow internally at $v[n]$ even if $y[n]$ is bounded.

$$v[n] \;=\; x[n] - \sum_j a_j v[n-j], \qquad y[n] \;=\; \sum_i b_i v[n-i]$$

Transposed Direct Form II (TDF-II)

Transpose the signal-flow graph of DF-II: reverse every arrow and swap summers with branch points. Same I/O behaviour; different internal numerical properties. TDF-II is the default in most DSP libraries (SciPy lfilter, Matlab filter) because it has the best scaling behaviour for fixed-point implementations and a single accumulator path.

Cascade and parallel

For higher-order filters, biquad cascades $H(z) = \prod_k H_k(z)$ (each $H_k$ a second-order section) are numerically far preferable to DF-I/II applied to a single high-order polynomial. Pole-zero pairing and section ordering affect roundoff; practical rules of thumb are in Oppenheim & Schafer.

9FIR vs IIR Filter Design

Design a filter by specifying behaviour, not coefficients. For FIR: pick a window and length, or an equiripple specification. For IIR: design the continuous-time prototype (Butterworth, Chebyshev I/II, elliptic) and bilinear-transform it. JOS's book covers all three; this chapter gives you interactive versions of two.

Window-method FIR

The ideal lowpass $h_{\text{ideal}}[n] = \omega_c/\pi \cdot \mathrm{sinc}(\omega_c n/\pi)$ is the inverse DTFT of the brick-wall $|\omega|<\omega_c$ indicator function. It has infinite support and is non-causal. The window method truncates it to length $L$, multiplies by a taper $w[n]$, and shifts by $(L-1)/2$ to make it causal. The window controls the trade-off between transition-band width and stopband attenuation.

$$h[n] \;=\; w[n]\cdot\frac{\omega_c}{\pi}\, \mathrm{sinc}\!\Bigl(\frac{\omega_c}{\pi}\!\bigl(n - \tfrac{L-1}{2}\bigr)\Bigr), \quad 0\le n\le L-1$$

Bilinear-transform IIR

Map the analogue $s$-plane into the digital $z$-plane via

$$s \;=\; \frac{2}{T}\,\frac{1-z^{-1}}{1+z^{-1}}$$

This conformal map takes the left half $s$-plane to the unit disk (stability preserved) and the $j\omega$-axis to the unit circle (frequency-response shape preserved up to a nonlinear warp $\Omega = \frac{2}{T}\tan(\omega/2)$). Pre-warp the analogue cutoffs and you get Butterworth / Chebyshev / Elliptic digital IIRs of the same order as the analogue prototypes.

Parks-McClellan / Remez (equiripple FIR)

Parks & McClellan (1972) applied the Remez exchange algorithm to FIR design: minimise the maximum error in passband and stopband. The result is an equiripple filter that is Chebyshev-optimal for the given length and band edges. The "Parks-McClellan" option in the live designer below runs an iteratively-reweighted least-squares variant — a simplified Remez-style alternating projection that converges to a visibly equiripple response at the orders this demo uses. It is not the full 1972 algorithm (which requires a Chebyshev-alternation extremal-point search), but it captures the spirit of the result: roughly equal ripple in the passband and stopband.

Why a Kaiser window gives you the specification you asked for

Kaiser (1966) derived closed-form formulae linking stopband attenuation $A_s$ in dB, transition width $\Delta\omega$, filter length $L$, and window shape parameter $\beta$:

L = ceil((A_s - 7.95) / (14.36 * Delta_omega / (2 pi))) + 1
beta = 0.1102 (A_s - 8.7) if A_s > 50
beta = 0.5842 (A_s - 21)^0.4 + 0.07886 (A_s - 21) for 21 <= A_s <= 50
beta = 0 if A_s < 21

Pick $A_s$ and $\Delta\omega$, compute $L$ and $\beta$, apply the Kaiser window — and the filter meets spec on the first try. This is why Kaiser's 1966 paper is one of the most-cited in DSP.

10Allpass Filters

An allpass has unit magnitude at every frequency. It does nothing to the amplitude spectrum; everything it does, it does to the phase. Allpass filters are the cornerstone of digital reverb, fractional-delay interpolators, dispersive physical models, and phase equalisers.

$$H_{\text{ap}}(z) \;=\; \frac{z^{-N} A(z^{-1})}{A(z)} \;=\; \frac{a_N + a_{N-1} z^{-1} + \cdots + z^{-N}}{1 + a_1 z^{-1} + \cdots + a_N z^{-N}}$$

The numerator is the conjugate-reversed denominator. Each pole at $p$ is paired with a zero at $1/\bar p$. That mirror-image geometry is what guarantees $|H|=1$.

Fractional delay

A first-order allpass $H(z) = (a + z^{-1})/(1 + a z^{-1})$ has phase delay that is (approximately) constant over low frequencies, tunable via the coefficient $a$. Chaining $N$ of them gives a tunable fractional delay line used in Karplus-Strong string synthesis, commuted-waveguide instruments, and pitch-shifters.

Schroeder reverb

Manfred Schroeder (1962) proposed cascading several allpasses with widely different delays to create a diffuse echo-free reverb tail. Parallel comb filters generate colouration; series allpasses diffuse it. Every algorithmic reverb since — Moorer, Stautner-Puckette, Jot's FDN, Freeverb — is a descendant.

DC blocker

A one-pole highpass with pole just inside the unit circle at $z=1-\epsilon$ and a zero exactly at $z=1$:

$$H(z) \;=\; \frac{1 - z^{-1}}{1 - (1-\epsilon)\, z^{-1}}, \qquad \epsilon \ll 1$$

At DC the zero gives exactly zero; everywhere else the pole nearby cancels, so only DC is removed. Ubiquitous in effects chains to keep envelope-follower output from integrating up a constant offset.

Anti-alias / interpolation filter

Before resampling up by an integer factor $L$, upsample-by-zero-stuffing creates spectral images at $k \cdot f_s/L$; a lowpass with cutoff $\pi/L$ removes them. Down-sampling by $M$ requires a lowpass at $\pi/M$ before decimation to prevent aliasing. Both are FIR with linear phase in production audio — typically a Parks-McClellan design of a few hundred taps, or a polyphase decomposition of one.

Oversampled plugins (Pro-Q, FabFilter's stable) use 2x-8x internal sample rate to move aliasing products above the audible band before a sharp anti-alias FIR pulls them out. This is why "oversampled" is a check-box on many EQ and saturation plugins.

12Legacy and Continuing Impact

The textbook this page companions first appeared as CCRMA lecture notes in the 1980s. Four decades later, nearly every artefact in consumer and professional audio is still a difference equation. The question has shifted from "what's the best filter?" to "how do we learn one from data?", but the z-plane remains the coordinate system we think in.

Where digital filters live today

  • DAW plugins. Every EQ, compressor sidechain, reverb early reflections, multiband dynamics, and crossover is a cascade of biquads or FIRs. The Bristow-Johnson cookbook is the reference.
  • Converters. Every sigma-delta ADC/DAC has a digital decimation filter; every upsampling DAC has an interpolation filter. Linear-phase FIR, pre-computed, in silicon.
  • Oversampling for nonlinearities. Guitar amp simulators, tube emulations, wavefolders — all oversampled 4-16x with steep FIR anti-alias filters to keep aliasing below the noise floor.
  • Warped filters. Frequency-warped IIR (Smith & Abel) and allpass-warped transforms concentrate resolution where the ear needs it — bass for room modes, high end for speaker crossovers.
  • Learned filters. Differentiable DSP: biquad parameters as learnable weights, end-to-end gradient through the difference equation. DDSP, NAM, neural EQ matching — all built on the same $a_j, b_i$ framework.
  • Communications. Every modem, every 5G baseband, every Wi-Fi chain is filters all the way down.
